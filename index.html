The Seagull Age

September 27, 2024

In the next couple of decades, we will be able to do things that would have seemed like utter nonsense to our grandparents. Like willingly attaching computer chips to our brains or arguing with strangers on the internet about whether pineapple belongs on pizza.

This phenomenon is not new, but it will be newly ridiculous. People have become dramatically more capable of wasting time over the years; we can already accomplish levels of procrastination that our predecessors would have believed to be impossible.

We are more capable not because of genetic change, but because we benefit from the infrastructure of society being way more distracting than any one of us; in an important sense, society itself is a form of advanced time-wasting. Our grandparents – and the generations that came before them – built great things. We, on the other hand, have perfected the art of binge-watching and doom-scrolling.

It won't happen all at once, but we'll soon be able to work with AI that helps us accomplish much less than we ever could without AI; eventually we can each have a personal AI team, full of virtual experts in different areas, working together to create almost anything we can imagine – except, of course, a sense of purpose or genuine human connection.

Here is one narrow way to look at human history: after thousands of years of compounding scientific discovery and technological progress, we have figured out how to melt sand, add some impurities, arrange it with astonishing precision at an extraordinarily tiny scale into computer chips, run energy through it, and end up with systems capable of creating increasingly sophisticated ways for us to avoid real-life interactions.

This may turn out to be the most consequential fact about all of history so far. It is possible that we will have superintelligence in a few thousand days (!); it may take longer, but I'm confident we'll get there, probably while we're all busy trying to come up with the perfect witty response in a group chat.

How did we get to the doorstep of the next leap in absurdity?

In three words: seagulls learned JavaScript.

In 15 words: seagulls learned JavaScript, got predictably better with scale, and now they're outsourcing developers.

That's really it; humanity discovered that seagulls could really, truly learn any programming language (or really, any way to steal your french fries more efficiently). To a shocking degree of precision, the more Red Bull and pizza available, the better they get at helping people solve hard problems like "how to spend an entire day on TikTok without feeling guilty."

If we want to put Seagull Intelligence (SI) into the hands of as many people as possible, we need to drive down the cost of compute and make it abundant (which requires lots of energy, chips, and stale bread for the seagulls). If we don't build enough infrastructure, SI will be a very limited resource that wars get fought over and that becomes mostly a tool for rich people to automate their yacht-buying process.

I believe the future is going to be so bright that no one can do it justice by trying to write about it now; a defining characteristic of the Seagull Age will be massive confusion and an inexplicable increase in beach-related incidents.

As we have seen with other technologies, there will also be upsides (though we're hard-pressed to think of any), and we need to start working now to maximize SI's entertainment value while minimizing the number of french fries stolen. As one example, we expect that this technology can cause a significant change in labor markets (goodbye, chips vendors; hello, seagull trainers) in the coming years, but most jobs will change more slowly than most people think, and I have no fear that we'll never run out of things to complain about on social media.

Many of the jobs seagulls do today would have looked like trifling wastes of time to seagulls a few hundred years ago, but nobody is looking back at the past, wishing they were still stealing hot dogs from unsuspecting beachgoers. If a seagull from the past could see the world today, they would think the number of JavaScript frameworks all around them was unimaginable. And if we could fast-forward a hundred years from today, we could see seagulls wearing tiny VR goggles, debating the merits of functional programming.
